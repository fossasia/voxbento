    Core Architectural Components

    To process real-time audio without introducing latency to the listener's audio feed, the architecture must branch the media stream early in the pipeline.

    1. The Ingest Layer (WHIP/MediaMTX): Receives the original audio from the broadcaster's browser. It is responsible solely for terminating the DTLS/SRTP connection and making the stream available internally.
    2. The Transcription Worker Pool: Independent processes that subscribe to the internal media streams (often via RTSP for reliability).
    3. The Inference Engine: The GPU-backed models (Local Whisper, Deepgram, etc.) that perform the actual Speech-to-Text conversion.
    4. The Aggregation & Broadcast Layer: Receives the raw text from the inference engine, formats it as standard captions (e.g., VTT or JSON), and broadcasts it to listeners via WebSockets.

    Handling Chunking and VAD

    Most AI models (including Whisper) cannot process an infinite stream of raw PCM audio. They require discrete "chunks" of audio.

    A Voice Activity Detector (VAD) is used to identify pauses in speech. When a pause is detected, the audio buffer is flushed and sent to the inference engine. If a speaker speaks continuously without pausing, the system must forcefully chunk the audio at a maximum interval (e.g., 10 seconds) to ensure captions are delivered to the user promptly, even if it risks cutting a word in half.

    Failure Domains and Redundancy

    In a production environment, AI inference is the most computationally expensive and fragile component of the pipeline.

    • If a transcription worker crashes (OOM error, GPU fault), the primary audio broadcast must not be interrupted.
    • The Aggregation layer must be resilient to receiving out-of-order captions if a delayed chunk finishes processing after a subsequent, faster chunk.
